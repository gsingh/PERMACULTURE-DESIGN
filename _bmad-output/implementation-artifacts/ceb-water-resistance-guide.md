## WHY CEBs ABSORB WATER (The Science)

### CEB Composition:

**Typical Stabilized CEB:**
- 70-80% **Soil** (clay 15-25% + sand 60-80% + silt 10-20%)
- 8-12% **Cement or Lime** (stabilizer/binder)
- 8-10% **Water** (for mixing, evaporates during curing)

### The Problem: Clay is Hydrophilic (Attracts Water)

**What happens when CEB gets wet:**

1. **Water penetrates** through microscopic pores (capillary action)
2. **Clay particles swell** (expand 10-40% volume when saturated)
3. **Block softens** (clay loses cohesion, becomes sticky)
4. **Surface erosion** begins (if rainwater flows over surface)
5. **Structural weakness** develops (load-bearing capacity decreases)

**The Solution: Stabilization + Protection**

**Cement stabilization:**
- Cement hydrates (reacts with water) → forms calcium silicate hydrate crystals
- Crystals create waterproof matrix around soil particles
- Blocks water penetration, maintains strength when wet

**Design protection:**
- Prevent water from reaching CEB wall in the first place
- Even best stabilized CEB should NOT be constantly exposed to rain

### Stabilization Method 2: **Lime Stabilization** (Traditional, Eco-Friendly Alternative)

**Lime Type:** Hydrated lime (calcium hydroxide) - "चूना" (chuna) in Hindi

**Percentage:** 8-12% by weight of soil (similar to cement)

**Advantages Over Cement:**
- ✅ More breathable (allows moisture vapor to pass - healthier indoor air)
- ✅ Better thermal mass (5-10% better insulation value)
- ✅ Self-healing (lime re-carbonates over time, seals hairline cracks)
- ✅ Lower carbon footprint (60-80% less CO₂ than cement production)
- ✅ Traditional (used in Indian architecture for 1,000+ years)

**Disadvantages:**
- ⚠️ Slower strength gain (28 days full cure vs 7 days for cement)
- ⚠️ Lower compressive strength (20-30% less than cement-stabilized)
- ⚠️ Harder to source (not as common as cement in rural India)
- ⚠️ Requires more skill (mixing, curing process more sensitive)

**Recommendation:** 
- **Use lime if:** You can source quality hydrated lime locally + have experienced CEB manufacturer
- **Use cement if:** Lime not available OR first-time CEB construction (cement is more forgiving)

### Stabilization Method 3: **Cement + Lime Hybrid** ⭐ (BEST OF BOTH WORLDS)

**Mix Ratio:**
- 5-6% Cement (fast initial strength, good binding)
- 4-5% Lime (breathability, thermal performance, long-term durability)

**Benefits:**
- ✅ Fast strength gain (cement gives 70-80% strength in 7 days)
- ✅ Long-term improvement (lime continues hardening for years/decades)
- ✅ Good water resistance (cement) + breathability (lime)
- ✅ Cost-effective (use less cement, supplement with cheaper lime)
- ✅ Best thermal performance (lime contribution)

**This is the IDEAL solution for your permaculture farm if both materials are available**

**Cost Impact:**
- Cement (5%): ₹1.5/block
- Lime (5%): ₹1/block
- **Total:** ₹2.5/block (same as 10% cement, but BETTER performance)

### CEB Manufacturing Quality Control Checklist

**1. Soil Testing (BEFORE Production Begins):**

✅ **Soil Composition Test (Jar Test - DIY Method):**
- Fill clear jar 1/3 with soil sample
- Add water to 2/3 full
- Add 1 teaspoon salt (helps particles settle)
- Shake vigorously for 3 minutes
- Let settle for 24 hours
- Observe layers:
  - Bottom layer (settles first): **Sand** (should be 60-80%)
  - Middle layer: **Silt** (should be 10-20%)
  - Top layer (settles last): **Clay** (should be 15-25%)
  - Floating debris: **Organic matter** (should be <5%)

**IDEAL Kurukshetra soil for CEB:**
- Sand: 60-75% ✅
- Silt: 10-20% ✅
- Clay: 15-25% ✅
- Organic matter: <5% ✅

**If clay >30%:** Add sand to mix (reduces water absorption)  
**If clay <10%:** Add clay OR increase stabilizer to 12% (more binding needed)

---

✅ **Compression Test (Professional - Recommended):**
- Send soil sample to civil engineering lab (check Kurukshetra University or private lab)
- Tests: Grain size distribution, Atterberg limits, compaction test
- Cost: ₹3,000-5,000 (one-time, worth it for 8,000-10,000 blocks per house!)

---

**2. Block Production (Quality Standards):**

✅ **Moisture Content (Critical!):**
- Test: Squeeze handful of soil-cement mix
- Correct moisture: Forms ball, doesn't drip water, crumbles when dropped from 3 ft height
- Too wet: Blocks will crack during drying
- Too dry: Poor compaction, weak blocks

✅ **Compression Pressure:**
- Minimum: 2,000 PSI (140 kg/cm²) - requires hydraulic press
- Hand-operated lever press: NOT sufficient (only 500-800 PSI)
- **MUST use hydraulic press** for exterior-grade stabilized CEBs

✅ **Block Dimensions (Standard):**
- Length: 9 inches (230mm)
- Width: 4.5 inches (115mm)
- Height: 3 inches (75mm)
- Tolerance: ±3mm (blocks must be uniform for good mortar joints)

✅ **Surface Quality:**
- No cracks (hairline cracks OK, but deep cracks = reject)
- Smooth surfaces (no crumbling edges)
- Sharp corners (not rounded or chipped)

---

**3. Curing Process (MOST CRITICAL for Strength!):**

**Cement-Stabilized CEBs:**

**Day 1 (Immediately After Pressing):**
- Remove blocks from press gently (don't drop!)
- Stack on flat surface (wood pallets or concrete floor)
- Cover with wet burlap or plastic sheet (prevent rapid moisture loss)

**Days 2-7 (Wet Curing):**
- Spray blocks with water 3-4 times daily (morning, midday, evening, night if possible)
- Keep covered with wet burlap between spraying
- NEVER let blocks dry out (cement needs water to hydrate!)

**Days 8-14 (Air Curing):**
- Continue spraying 1-2 times daily
- Can reduce covering (allow some air circulation)

**Days 15-28 (Final Curing):**
- No spraying needed (cement has gained 90%+ strength)
- Store in covered area (protect from rain, direct sun)

**Day 28:**
- Blocks ready for construction (100% design strength)

**SHORTCUTS TO AVOID:**
- ❌ Curing <7 days (blocks will be weak, absorb more water)
- ❌ Letting blocks dry out during curing (cement won't fully hydrate)
- ❌ Stacking wet blocks (causes deformation)

---

**Lime-Stabilized CEBs:**

**Similar to cement, but:**
- Wet curing: 14 days minimum (lime reacts slower)
- Air curing: 28-90 days (lime continues hardening for months)
- Use blocks after 28 days minimum, but strength continues improving for 6-12 months

---

**4. Strength Testing (Quality Assurance):**

**Sample Testing:**
- Test 5% of blocks (random sampling)
- Example: 10,000 blocks total → test 500 blocks

**Compression Test:**
- Age: 7 days (cement) or 28 days (lime)
- Method: Place block in compression testing machine, load until failure
- **Minimum required strength:** 80 kg/cm² (1,138 PSI) for exterior walls

**Accept/Reject Criteria:**
- **All samples ≥80 kg/cm²:** ✅ Accept entire batch
- **Any sample <60 kg/cm²:** ❌ Reject entire batch (insufficient stabilization)
- **Some samples 60-80 kg/cm²:** Investigate (possibly use for interior walls, not exterior)

**Where to test:** Civil engineering labs (Kurukshetra University, private labs in Ambala/Chandigarh)

**Cost:** ₹50-100 per block tested (₹2,500-5,000 for 50-block sample) - WORTH IT!

## PART 2: DESIGN PROTECTIONS (ESSENTIAL!)

### Even perfectly stabilized CEBs WILL FAIL without proper design. Follow ALL these rules:

### Protection 1: **ROOF OVERHANGS** ⭐ (MOST IMPORTANT!)

**What is Overhang?**
- Horizontal distance from wall face to roof edge
- Acts as umbrella for walls (sheds rain away from wall)

**Minimum Requirements for Kurukshetra:**

| Wall Side | Minimum Overhang | Recommended Overhang | Why |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------|-----|
| **South (Front)** | 2 ft (24 in) | **2.5-3 ft** ⭐ | Most rain exposure (monsoon from SW), most visible |
| **North (Back)** | 1.5 ft (18 in) | **2 ft** | Less rain, but needs protection |
| **East** | 2 ft (24 in) | **2.5 ft** | Morning sun + rain |
| **West** | 2 ft (24 in) | **2.5-3 ft** ⭐ | Afternoon sun (intense), rain |

**Math: How Overhang Protects Wall**

```
Monsoon rain angle: 30-45° from vertical (wind-driven)

2 ft overhang protects:
- At 30° angle: 2 ft ÷ tan(30°) = 3.5 ft of wall height
- At 45° angle: 2 ft ÷ tan(45°) = 2 ft of wall height

3 ft overhang protects:
- At 30° angle: 3 ft ÷ tan(30°) = 5.2 ft of wall height
- At 45° angle: 3 ft ÷ tan(45°) = 3 ft of wall height

For 10 ft wall height, 2.5-3 ft overhang is IDEAL
```

**Cost to Increase Overhang (from 1.5 ft to 3 ft):**

Per house:
- Additional roofing area: 116 ft perimeter × 1.5 ft extra = 174 sq ft
- Metal roofing cost: ₹80-120/sq ft × 174 = ₹13,920-20,880
- Additional roof frame: ₹3,000-5,000

**Total per house:** ₹17,000-26,000  
**Per family:** ₹4,250-6,500

**THIS IS THE SINGLE BEST INVESTMENT FOR CEB LONGEVITY!**

**ROI:**
- Cost: ₹4,250-6,500 (one-time)
- Saves: ₹5,000-10,000/year in plaster repairs (30-year savings = ₹1,50,000-3,00,000)
- **Payback: <1 year**

---

**Additional Overhang Features:**

✅ **Drip Edge:**
- Metal flashing at roof edge (L-shaped profile)
- Projects water 3-6 inches beyond wall
- Prevents water from running down wall face
- Cost: ₹20-40/running ft (₹2,320-4,640 per house)

✅ **Gutters (Highly Recommended):**
- Collect roof runoff, channel to storage or drain
- Prevents water pooling near foundation
- Doubles as rainwater harvesting (see swale system integration)
- Cost: ₹60-100/running ft (₹6,960-11,600 per house)

### Protection 2: **RAISED FOUNDATION** ⭐ (CRITICAL!)

**The Problem: Capillary Rise**

Ground moisture wicks up into porous materials (like CEB) through tiny capillaries
- Rising damp can climb 2-4 ft above ground level
- Bottom of CEB wall stays permanently damp
- Result: Erosion, algae growth, structural weakness, efflorescence (white salt deposits)

**The Solution: Raise CEB wall above ground moisture zone**

**Minimum Plinth Height:**

| Soil Type | Minimum Height | Recommended Height |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Sandy soil (well-drained)** | 12 inches | **18 inches** |
| **Loamy soil (Kurukshetra typical)** | 18 inches | **24 inches** ⭐ |
| **Clay soil (poor drainage)** | 24 inches | **30 inches** |

**For your farm: 18-24 inches MINIMUM (closer to 24 inches is better)**

---

**Foundation Material (Below CEB Wall):**

**❌ DO NOT USE CEBs below ground or in high-moisture areas**

**✅ USE waterproof materials:**

**Option 1: RCC (Reinforced Cement Concrete)** - RECOMMENDED
- Mix: M15 grade minimum (1 cement : 2 sand : 4 aggregate)
- Steel: 8mm bars, 6-inch spacing (mesh reinforcement)
- Thickness: 6-9 inches
- Height: 18-24 inches above ground + 12-18 inches below ground
- Cost: ₹200-300/running ft

**Option 2: Fired Brick**
- Type: First-class engineering bricks (water absorption <10%)
- Mortar: Cement-sand (1:4)
- Thickness: 9 inches (same as CEB wall above)
- Height: 18-24 inches above ground
- Cost: ₹150-250/running ft

**Option 3: Stone Masonry**
- Type: Local stone (if available near Kurukshetra - check quarries)
- Mortar: Cement-sand (1:4)
- Thickness: 12-15 inches (stone is irregular)
- Height: 18-24 inches above ground
- Cost: ₹180-280/running ft (depends on stone availability)

**For your 4 houses:**
- Perimeter per house: 116 ft
- Total: 464 running ft
- Cost: ₹69,600-1,39,200 total (₹17,400-34,800 per family)

**This is ESSENTIAL - already included in Phase 2 budget (foundation line item)**

---

**Damp-Proof Course (DPC):**

**What:** Waterproof layer between foundation and CEB wall (prevents moisture wicking upward)

**Material Options:**

**Option 1: Plastic Sheet (Polythene)** - CHEAPEST
- Thickness: 1000 gauge (250 microns)
- Cost: ₹10-15/sq ft

- Thickness: 1/4 inch
- Cost: ₹30-50/sq ft
- Installation: Apply to foundation top, let cure 24 hours before starting CEB wall

**Recommendation for your farm: Option 3** (cement slurry with waterproofing)

### Protection 3: **EXTERIOR PLASTER** ⭐ (ESSENTIAL!)

**Even 12% cement-stabilized CEB should be plastered on exterior**

**Why:**
- Creates continuous waterproof barrier (no joints = no water entry points)
- Protects CEB from UV degradation (sunlight breaks down cement over time)
- Provides aesthetic finish (smooth, paintable surface)
- Allows minor repairs (easier to patch plaster than replace CEB)

**Plaster Type Options:**

---

**Option 1: Lime Plaster** ⭐ (RECOMMENDED for Permaculture Farm)

**Mix Ratio:** 1 part lime : 3 parts sand (by volume)

**Lime Type:** 
- Hydrated lime (चूना - chuna) OR
- Fat lime (putty lime - if available, BETTER quality)

**Sand:**
- River sand (sharp sand, not desert sand)
- Grain size: Mix of fine (30%), medium (50%), coarse (20%)
- Clean (no clay, organic matter, salt)

**Application:**

**Base Coat (Scratch Coat):**
- Thickness: 10-12mm (3/8 - 1/2 inch)
- Wet CEB surface before applying (spray water)
- Apply plaster, press firmly to fill all joints
- Scratch surface with comb/nail (creates grooves for next coat to grip)
- Cure: Spray water 2x daily for 7 days

**Finish Coat:**
- Wait 7 days after base coat
- Thickness: 5-8mm (1/4 inch)
- Finer sand (or sieve base coat sand)
- Apply smooth, trowel finish
- Cure: Spray water 2x daily for 7 days

**Total Plaster Thickness:** 15-20mm (5/8 - 3/4 inch)

**Advantages:**
- ✅ Breathable (allows moisture vapor to pass - prevents trapped moisture)
- ✅ Flexible (accommodates minor CEB movement, reduces cracking)
- ✅ Self-healing (lime re-carbonates, seals hairline cracks over time)
- ✅ Beautiful (warm, natural finish)
- ✅ Low embodied energy (sustainable)
- ✅ Traditional (proven in Indian architecture for 1,000+ years)

**Disadvantages:**
- ⚠️ Slower curing (28 days full strength vs 7 days for cement)
- ⚠️ Softer surface (can scratch/dent more easily than cement plaster)
- ⚠️ Requires skill (mixing, application technique more sensitive)

**Cost:**
- Materials: ₹30-45/sq ft (lime + sand + labor)
- Per house (exterior only, ~1,400 sq ft): ₹42,000-63,000
- **Total 4 houses:** ₹1,68,000-2,52,000 (₹42,000-63,000 per family)

**Advantages:**

**Cost:**

### Protection 4: **DRAINAGE AROUND FOUNDATION** ⭐ (HIGHLY RECOMMENDED)

**The Problem:**

Monsoon rain + poor site grading = water pools around house foundation
- Water saturates foundation
- Moisture wicks up into CEB wall (even with DPC, water pressure can overcome DPC)
- Basement/foundation movement (if soil expands when wet)

**The Solution: Perimeter Drainage**

---

**Option 1: Gravel Bed (Simple, Effective)** ⭐ RECOMMENDED

**Design:**
```
House Wall
    |
    |─── CEB Wall
    |
    └─── Raised Foundation (18-24 in)
         |
         |
━━━━━━━━┷━━━━━━━━━  Ground Level
         ┃
         ┃ Gravel Bed
         ┃ (12 in wide × 12 in deep)
         ┃ 3/4 inch crushed stone
         ┃
         ┃ Slope: 1% grade away from house
         ┃ (1 inch drop per 10 feet)
         ┗━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━→ to garden/swale
```

**Construction:**
1. Excavate trench: 12 inches wide × 12 inches deep, all around house perimeter
2. Line bottom with landscape fabric (prevents soil mixing with gravel)
3. Fill with 3/4 inch crushed stone (NOT sand - sand clogs)
4. Top with 2 inches soil (allows grass to grow over for aesthetics)
5. Slope trench away from house: 1% grade minimum (1 inch drop per 10 feet)

**Outlet:**
- Gravel bed drains to garden area OR swale (50+ ft from house)
- Natural infiltration (water soaks into ground 10-20 ft from foundation)

**Materials per house:**
- Trench: 116 ft perimeter × 1 ft wide × 1 ft deep = 116 cubic ft = 4.3 cubic yards
- Gravel (3/4 inch crushed stone): 4.3 cu yd × ₹800/cu yd = ₹3,440
- Landscape fabric: 116 ft × 1 ft = 116 sq ft × ₹5/sq ft = ₹580
- Labor: 1-2 days × ₹500/day = ₹500-1,000

**Total per house:** ₹4,520-5,020  
**Total 4 houses:** ₹18,080-20,080 (₹4,520-5,020 per family)

**Option 2: French Drain (More Effective, More Expensive)**

**Design:**
```
House Wall
    |
    |─── CEB Wall
    |
    └─── Raised Foundation
         |
━━━━━━━━┷━━━━━━━━━  Ground Level
         ┃
         ┃ ┌─────────────┐
         ┃ │   Gravel    │
         ┃ │             │
         ┃ │ [Perforated │
         ┃ │  PVC Pipe]  │ 4-inch diameter
         ┃ │             │
         ┃ └─────────────┘
         ┃
         ┗━━━━━━━━━━━━━━→ Outlet pipe to swale/garden
```

**Construction:**
1. Excavate trench: 12 inches wide × 18 inches deep
2. Line with landscape fabric
3. Add 3 inches gravel at bottom
4. Lay 4-inch perforated PVC pipe (holes facing down)
5. Cover pipe with gravel (fill to 3 inches below ground)
6. Fold landscape fabric over top
7. Add 3 inches soil on top
8. Slope pipe: 1% grade minimum (1 inch drop per 10 feet)

**Outlet:**
- Pipe exits 50-100 ft from house, outlets to swale or garden
- Can also connect to stormwater system (if available)

**Advantages over gravel bed:**
- Faster drainage (pipe carries water away quickly)
- Better for high water table areas
- Can handle larger volume of water (heavy monsoon)

**Materials per house:**
- Gravel: 6 cu yd × ₹800 = ₹4,800
- 4-inch perforated pipe: 116 ft × ₹40/ft = ₹4,640
- Landscape fabric: 232 sq ft × ₹5/sq ft = ₹1,160
- Labor: 2-3 days × ₹500/day = ₹1,000-1,500

**Total per house:** ₹11,600-12,100  
**Total 4 houses:** ₹46,400-48,400 (₹11,600-12,100 per family)

**Use Option 1 (Gravel Bed)** for Kurukshetra climate:

### Protection 5: **EXTERIOR PAINT/SEALANT** (RECOMMENDED)

**After plastering, add paint or sealant for extra protection**

**Option 1: Lime Wash** ⭐ (Traditional, Breathable)

**What:** Lime + water + natural pigments

**Mix:**
- 1 kg slaked lime (hydrated lime putty)
- 3-4 liters water
- Optional: Natural pigments (iron oxide for red/yellow, charcoal for black)

### Case Study 4: **Failed CEB Projects (What NOT to Do)**

**Location:** Various (rural India)

**Common Failure Modes:**

**Failure 1: Unstabilized CEBs in High Rainfall**
- No cement/lime added (pure earth blocks)
- Result: Erosion after 2-3 monsoons, walls softened, eventually collapsed
- **Lesson:** MUST stabilize with minimum 8% cement for monsoon climates

**Failure 2: Poor Roof Overhangs**
- Overhang <1 ft (minimal protection)

- **Lesson:** 2-3 ft overhangs are ESSENTIAL, not optional

**Failure 3: No Raised Foundation**
- CEB walls started at ground level (no plinth)
- Result: Bottom 1-2 ft of wall stayed damp, algae growth, structural weakness at base
- **Lesson:** MUST raise CEB wall at least 18 inches above ground

**Failure 4: No Plaster (Exposed Stabilized CEB)**
- Left CEB exposed (no plaster) for "natural look"
- Result: Surface erosion after 5-10 years, especially on south/west walls (sun + rain exposure)
- **Lesson:** MUST plaster exterior walls, even if CEB is well-stabilized

**Common Thread:** ALL failures were due to **design mistakes**, NOT material failure of CEB itself

## PART 7: FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

### Q1: "Can I skip stabilization if I plaster immediately?"

**A:** ❌ **NO - This is dangerous**

Even with plaster:
- Plaster can crack (especially around openings, corners)
- Cracks expose CEB to water
- Unstabilized CEB will erode rapidly through cracks
- Within 3-5 years, you'll have major structural problems

**Stabilization is NON-NEGOTIABLE for monsoon climates**

---

### Q2: "Can I use less than 10% cement to save money?"

**A:** ⚠️ **Not recommended for Kurukshetra**

6-8% cement:
- Works in DRY climates (Rajasthan, dry zones of Gujarat)
- RISKY in monsoon climates (450-600mm rainfall)
- Water resistance is marginal (8-15% absorption vs 5-10% for 10% cement)

**Save money elsewhere (interior walls can use 6% cement), but NOT on exterior walls**

---

### Q3: "Can I leave stabilized CEB exposed (no plaster) for natural look?"

**A:** ⚠️ **Only in very specific conditions**

Exposed stabilized CEB works IF:
- ✅ 12-15% cement stabilization (higher than normal)
- ✅ HUGE roof overhangs (4-5 ft minimum)
- ✅ Dry climate OR building in rain shadow
- ✅ You accept that surface will erode/stain over 10-20 years (aesthetic issue, not structural)

**For Kurukshetra: NOT recommended**
- Monsoon rain will stain exposed CEB (even if stabilized)
- Sun + rain cycles degrade surface faster than plastered wall
- Plaster is cheap (₹40-60/sq ft) - why risk it?

---

### Q4: "Lime vs. cement plaster - which is better for CEB?"

**A:** **Lime plaster is BETTER for most applications**

**Lime plaster advantages:**
- ✅ Breathable (moisture vapor can escape from CEB wall)
- ✅ Flexible (accommodates minor CEB movement without cracking)
- ✅ Compatible (same material family as CEB - better adhesion)
- ✅ Self-healing (lime re-carbonates, seals hairline cracks over time)
- ✅ Beautiful (warm, natural finish perfect for permaculture aesthetic)

**Cement plaster only for:**
- Bathrooms/kitchen (wet areas need maximum water resistance)
- Areas with plumbing penetrations

---

### Q5: "How long will CEB house last?"

**A:** **50-100+ years with proper stabilization and maintenance**

**Factors affecting lifespan:**

**CEB Quality:**
- Well-stabilized (10% cement): 75-100+ years
- Poorly stabilized (0-5% cement): 10-20 years

**Design Protection:**
- Good overhangs + raised foundation + plaster: 75-100 years
- Poor design (no overhangs, ground-level, no plaster): 5-15 years

**Maintenance:**
- Regular inspection and minor repairs: 75-100 years
- Neglect: 15-30 years

**Your CEB houses (if built to spec in this document): 75+ year lifespan, SAME as fired brick**

### Q7: "CEB vs. Fired Brick - which is more water-resistant?"

**A:** **Fired brick is slightly better, but difference is SMALL with proper CEB design**

**Water Absorption:**
- Fired brick: 10-20% (after 24-hour immersion)
- Stabilized CEB (10% cement): 5-10%
- **CEB actually absorbs LESS water than brick!**

**Structural Strength When Wet:**
- Fired brick: Maintains 95-100% strength
- Stabilized CEB (10% cement): Maintains 90-95% strength
- **5-10% difference is negligible for residential building**

**Long-Term Durability (with plaster + overhang):**
- Fired brick: 75-150 years
- Stabilized CEB: 75-100 years
- **Essentially equivalent**

**Winner: TIE (both perform excellently when properly designed)**

**Choose CEB for:**
- ✅ Cost savings (20-30% cheaper)
- ✅ Thermal performance (3-6°C cooler)
- ✅ Sustainability (80% less embodied energy)
- ✅ Breathability (healthier indoor air)

**Choose Brick if:**
- You can't find quality CEB manufacturer
- You want absolute minimum maintenance (10-20% less over 30 years)
- Budget is not a concern (brick costs 20-30% more)
