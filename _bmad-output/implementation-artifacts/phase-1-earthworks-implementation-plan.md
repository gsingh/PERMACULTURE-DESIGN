## üß≠ SURVEYING & LEVELING GUIDE

### Why Level Matters

**Swales MUST be perfectly level across their width (E-W direction) because:**
- If one end is lower, ALL water flows there = uneven infiltration
- Low end gets waterlogged, high end stays dry
- Trees planted on high end will die from drought
- Entire swale system fails if levels are off

**Acceptable tolerance:** ¬±2 inches across 209 feet (¬±1% grade) - aim for ¬±1 inch

### Method 1: Hire Professional Surveyor (RECOMMENDED)

**Cost:** ‚Çπ10,000-15,000 for full property

**What they do:**
- Use laser level or theodolite to mark exact contour lines
- Mark swale locations with stakes every 10-20 feet
- Provide written elevation data
- Take 1-2 days

**When to hire:**
- If using excavator method (essential for speed)
- If no one in families has surveying experience
- If budget allows

### Method 2: DIY A-Frame Level (FREE - See detailed instructions)

**What is an A-frame level?**
- Simple wooden tool shaped like letter "A"
- Plumb line hangs from top, shows when ground is level
- Accurate to ¬±1 inch over 10 feet (good enough for swales)

**Materials needed (‚Çπ500-1,000):**
- 3 pieces of wood: Two 6ft legs, one 4ft crossbar
- String (3 feet)
- Weight (rock, bolt, anything heavy)
- Nails or screws
- Saw, drill (or hire carpenter for ‚Çπ500)

**How to build (30-60 minutes):**

```
Step 1: Create "A" shape
        /\
       /  \
      /    \
     /‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ\
    
- Legs: 6 feet long each
- Crossbar: 4 feet long, 2 feet from bottom
- Legs meet at top at 60-degree angle

Step 2: Attach plumb line
- Tie string to top (where legs meet)
- Hang weight from string
- Mark spot where string crosses crossbar when on level ground
- This is your "level mark"

Step 3: Test & calibrate
- Put A-frame on flat surface (floor)
- Note where plumb line hits crossbar
- Flip A-frame 180 degrees
- Plumb line should hit SAME spot
- If not, adjust legs until it does
```

**How to use A-frame:**
1. Place on ground along swale line
2. If plumb line is RIGHT of level mark = left leg is higher
3. If plumb line is LEFT of level mark = right leg is higher
4. Dig out high side until plumb line matches level mark
5. Move A-frame 6 feet, repeat
6. Continue across entire 209ft width

**Time required:** 2-3 hours per swale (slow but accurate)

### Method 3: Water Level (Simple, accurate)

**Materials needed (‚Çπ300-500):**
- Clear plastic tubing (3/4 inch diameter, 220+ feet long): ‚Çπ300-400
- Water
- 2 people

**How it works:**
- Fill tube with water (no air bubbles)
- Water surface at both ends will be at SAME elevation (gravity!)
- Person 1 holds one end at stake on West side
- Person 2 walks to East side with other end
- Mark where water level is at both ends = same elevation
- Move stakes, repeat every 10-20 feet

**Pros:** Very accurate, cheap, easy
**Cons:** Tube can kink, takes 2 people, slow

### Marking Contours

**Once you've determined level lines:**

1. **Stakes:** Pound wooden stakes (2-3 ft tall) every 10-20 feet along swale line
2. **String line:** Tie string between stakes at ground level (marks top edge of swale)
3. **Spray paint or lime powder:** Mark actual digging line on ground (3-4ft wide)
4. **Label stakes:** Write swale number on each stake ("S1", "S2", etc.)

**Photo documentation:** Take photos from North end showing all stakes in line (helps confirm)

## üíß WATER TESTING & SYSTEM VALIDATION

### Pre-Monsoon Testing (CRITICAL STEP)

**DO NOT SKIP THIS!** Test your system BEFORE first monsoon to avoid disasters.

**Test 1: Individual Swale Level Test**

**Method:**
1. Close overflow channel with temporary berm (pile soil, sandbags)
2. Fill swale with tubewell water (1-2 hours of pumping)
3. Observe water surface - is it level across width?
4. Measure depth at West end, center, East end (should be within 1-2 inches)
5. If one end deeper = dig out that end slightly
6. Repeat until level

**Pass criteria:** Water depth varies less than 2 inches across 209ft width

**Test 2: Infiltration Rate Test**

**Method:**
1. Fill swale completely with water
2. Mark water level
3. Return 24 hours later
4. Measure how much water infiltrated vs. evaporated

**Target rate:**
- **Clay-heavy soil:** 30-50% infiltration in 24 hours (rest evaporates)
- **Loamy soil:** 60-80% infiltration in 24 hours
- **Sandy soil:** 90-100% infiltration in 24 hours

**If infiltration is too slow (<20% in 24 hours):**
- Soil may be too compacted
- Use garden fork to loosen bottom of swale (don't dig, just poke holes)
- Add thin layer of compost to bottom (improves soil structure)
- Re-test

**Test 3: Overflow System Test**

**Method:**
1. Fill Swale 1 completely
2. Continue pumping water
3. Observe overflow - does it flow to Swale 2 through overflow channel?
4. Fill Swale 2, watch overflow to Swale 3
5. Continue testing cascade for Swales 1-4

**Pass criteria:**
- Overflow channels carry water without eroding
- Water doesn't flood around swale ends
- Each swale fills before overflowing to next (not bypassing)

**Test 4: Pond Fill & Hold Test**

**Method:**
1. Fill pond with tubewell water (may take several hours)
2. Mark water level
3. Wait 48-72 hours
4. Measure water loss

**Expected loss:**
- **Evaporation (summer):** 1/4 - 1/2 inch per day
- **Infiltration (unlined pond):** 1-2 inches per day initially (slows as clay seals)
- **TOTAL:** 2-3 inches per 48 hours acceptable

**If water drains too fast (>6 inches in 48 hours):**
- Soil may be too sandy/porous
- Solution: Add clay layer to bottom (6-12 inches thick, compact well)
- Or: Accept higher infiltration (recharges groundwater, just requires more refilling)

### Monsoon Observation (Year 1)

**During first monsoon, observe and document:**

- [ ] **Which swales fill first?** (Should be Swale 1, then 2, then 3...)
- [ ] **Do any overflow prematurely?** (May indicate level issue or channel too large)
- [ ] **Any erosion?** (Berms washing away, channels deepening)
- [ ] **Flooding near houses?** (Swale 1 not capturing enough)
- [ ] **Pond filling?** (Connected to swale system correctly?)

**Photo log:** Take photos during/after heavy rains (same locations each time)

**Adjustments (post-monsoon):**
- Repair any eroded areas
- Adjust overflow channel sizes if needed
- Add more mulch to berms
- Improve infiltration if water sits too long (>48 hours)

## üöß COMMON PROBLEMS & SOLUTIONS

### Problem 1: Swale Isn't Level Across Width

**Symptoms:** Water pools at one end, other end dry

**Causes:**
- Didn't check level carefully during digging
- Ground settled unevenly after digging
- Operator error (if excavator used)

**Solutions:**
- Use A-frame or water level to identify high/low spots
- Dig out high end to lower it, OR
- Fill low end with soil from berm
- Re-compact and re-test
- **Prevention:** Check level every 10-20 feet DURING digging, not just at end

### Problem 2: Water Overflows Too Quickly (Doesn't Infiltrate)

**Symptoms:** Swale fills and overflows in minutes during heavy rain

**Causes:**
- Soil too compacted (from excavator or foot traffic)
- Clay layer preventing infiltration
- Swale too shallow (not enough storage capacity)

**Solutions:**
- Loosen swale bottom with garden fork (poke holes without digging)
- Add 2-3 inch layer of compost to bottom (improves soil structure)
- Deepen swale by 6-12 inches if possible
- Plant deep-rooted plants on berm (roots break up compaction over time)
- **Accept it:** If soil is pure clay, infiltration will always be slow (water will eventually soak in over 2-3 days)

### Problem 3: Berm Erodes During Monsoon

**Symptoms:** Soil washing away from berm, exposing bare ground

**Causes:**
- Berm not compacted enough
- No mulch or plant cover
- Berm slope too steep

**Solutions:**
- Re-shape berm with gentler slope (4:1 ratio instead of 2:1)
- Compact by walking on it, watering, and walking again
- Apply thick mulch layer (6-8 inches) immediately
- Plant pioneer plants ASAP (roots stabilize soil)
- **Prevention:** Mulch and plant berms BEFORE first monsoon

### Problem 4: Pond Drains Too Fast

**Symptoms:** Pond empty within days of filling, faster than evaporation alone

**Causes:**
- Sandy/porous soil
- Underground leak (rock layer, crack)
- Pond sides too steep (collapse and seepage)

**Solutions:**
- Add clay layer to bottom (6-12 inches thick, compact with roller or by walking)
- Use "bentonite clay" (available at pond supply shops, seals leaks) - ‚Çπ5,000-10,000
- Let it self-seal: Often clay particles in water settle and seal bottom naturally after 2-3 fill cycles
- Accept higher infiltration rate (recharges groundwater, just requires more frequent refilling)
- **Prevention:** Test soil before digging (dig 3ft hole, fill with water, see how fast it drains)

### Problem 5: Excavator Damages More Than It Helps

**Symptoms:** Soil over-compacted, swales too deep/wide, berms destroyed

**Causes:**
- Inexperienced operator
- Wrong size excavator (too large)
- Poor communication with operator

**Solutions:**
- STOP immediately if operator isn't following plan
- Pay only for work completed correctly
- Switch to manual method for remaining swales
- Use other families' manual labor to fix damaged areas
- **Prevention:** 
  - Hire operator with swale experience (ask for references, photos of past work)
  - Walk through plan with operator BEFORE starting (show this document)
  - Designate one family member to supervise and guide operator throughout

### Problem 6: Families Can't Keep Up With Manual Labor

**Symptoms:** Exhaustion, slow progress, injuries, low morale

**Causes:**
- Underestimated difficulty
- Not enough people working
- Poor work schedule (too many hours, wrong time of day)

**Solutions:**
- Reduce hours per day (4 hours max instead of 8)
- Extend timeline (do 1 swale every 2 weeks instead of 1 per week)
- Hire 2-4 daily wage workers to supplement family labor (‚Çπ500/day each)
- Switch to hybrid method (hire excavator for remaining swales)
- Take week-long breaks between swales to recover
- **Prevention:** Start with realistic expectations, build in flexibility

### Problem 7: Neighbor Complaints (Noise, Dust, Blocked Access)

**Symptoms:** Neighbors upset about excavator noise, dust from digging

**Solutions:**
- Inform neighbors BEFORE starting (courtesy visit, explain benefits to area)
- Limit noisy work to acceptable hours (8 AM - 6 PM)
- Water down work area to reduce dust
- Offer neighbors some benefit (free fruit trees, use of pond, etc.)
- **Prevention:** Community engagement from the start

## üìÖ SEASONAL TIMING GUIDE

### Best Time to Dig: October - February (Post-Monsoon to Pre-Summer)

**Why:**
- ‚úÖ Soil is moist (easier to dig than dry summer soil)
- ‚úÖ Temperature moderate (20-30¬∞C, not extreme)
- ‚úÖ Work completed before next monsoon (June)
- ‚úÖ Time to test system with tubewell before first monsoon
- ‚úÖ Can plant trees on berms in Feb-March (monsoon will water them)

### Avoid: March - June (Summer)

**Why:**
- ‚ùå Extreme heat (40-45¬∞C) = health risks, low productivity
- ‚ùå Soil rock-hard (very difficult to dig)
- ‚ùå Less time before monsoon (rushed)

### Avoid: July - September (Monsoon)

**Why:**
- ‚ùå Muddy, slippery conditions (dangerous, can't work)
- ‚ùå Excavated soil turns to mud (can't build berms)
- ‚ùå May cause erosion/flooding if system incomplete
- ‚ùå Equipment gets stuck

**EXCEPTION:** If you MUST work in summer (families only available then):
- Work ONLY early morning (5-9 AM) before heat peaks
- Double water intake
- Accept slower progress
